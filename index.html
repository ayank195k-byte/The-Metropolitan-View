Essay No. 01 — Society & Adulthood

The Endless
Adolescence America's Most Comfortable Crisis

Why a generation old enough to vote, serve, and die for their country still isn't sure they're grown up, and why that question depends entirely on who's asking.

Local — Philadelphia

Philadelphia is a city filled with enigmas. It is home to America's founding fathers, home to some of the oldest institutions of higher education and some of its most visible poverty. It has a neighborhood for every tax bracket. Underfunded and filthy SEPTA trains for every hour of the night, this can be a painful reality for people like Miracle, a young woman thrown out of the foster care system who has been documented sleeping on those trains just to get away from all the shit and filth this city has to offer to its citizens (Volk, 2021). Philadelphia is also filled with recent college graduates, most of those products of the wealthy sitting dumbfounded and pondering cluelessly about what exactly it is they are supposed to do next. Megan (2008) documented this particular species in the Philadelphia Inquirer, about those who followed the easy path of completing college but having no idea what the world actually requires of them. The question that slowly crawls in the spine of all these young adults is almost identical: at what point exactly did I become an adult? And why can't anybody tell me the damn truth?

During a conversation, this question flows freely. Adulthood is a real and necessary path everyone has to cross, but no one ever tells us exactly how. Legally it is 18, neurologically it has long been claimed to be 25, although that claim is becoming difficult to defend as more research is published (Burnett, 2024). Emotional, social and financial maturity differs by who and where you are, and how willing your parents or guardians are to support you.

My Stake

I moved to Philadelphia a month ago. Not because I had to. Because everything in my body wanted to. I was done with the chains of living inside someone else's house, someone else's idea of what a life in progress is supposed to look like. I packed up, quit my job and moved in with my girlfriend, and started doing what adults apparently do.

Nobody tells you that adulthood shows you its colors through household supplies. Not through some cinematic or "ah-hah" moment but rather through trash bags, parking tickets and next month's rent. Seeing past the hidden veil of a functioning life costs actual money and requires actual coordination from an actual person, and that person is now you. It is not easy nor glamorous. It is also, and I want to emphasize this, significantly better than the alternative.

That last part is the whole point of this piece. Nobody tells you that independence, with all its ugly moments, all of its moving parts and weight, even standing in a completely new city with no idea how to pay next month's rent, is significantly better than the life where you are bound by the decisions of another person. This pride and accomplishment takes you from a "kid" to an "adult." After you take that leap everything after that lets you control the script of your own movie, where you are the director, the actor and the producer.

The countless wanderers I brush shoulders with on the streets of Philadelphia are not failed adults. They are a reminder that the question of when you grow up looks very different depending on what you're growing up into, and what you're growing up away from.

The Broader Picture

This question about adulthood is obviously not native to America. In the UK a study organized 722 people and asked them to describe adulthood. They all had a similar answer: for them adulthood was simply the feeling of having personal responsibility and the ability to survive, rather than the traditional goals of marriage or homeownership (Wright, 2023).

"Independence, with all its ugly moments and moving parts, is significantly better than the life where you are bound by the decisions of another person."

And then there is the ultimate final boss neuroscience-backed question which will surely put you in your place: "the brain isn't fully developed until 25." This ancient claim has been weaponized by the media and everyone from overly nosy parents to criminal defense attorneys. Except, as neuroscientist Dean Burnett (2024) explains, this is a total and absurd oversimplification of what the research actually says. The truth is that the brain continues changing throughout a person's entire life. What neuroscience has actually found is that certain regions associated with impulse control develop more slowly in adolescence, not that an 18-year-old is neurologically incapable of signing a lease or renting a car.

What this means is that we have curated a cultural and legal norm around adulthood that is convenient for some but a tragedy for others. We tell an 18-year-old they are old enough to vote, old enough to enlist, and then tell them their brain is not developed enough to be actually responsible. We cannot have it both ways. We must choose fairly and logically.

Who Gets Left Out
By the Numbers — Philadelphia

Approximately 20,000 young people age out of the foster care system in the United States every year. More than 30% will experience homelessness. In Philadelphia, many end up on SEPTA trains, not commuting, but surviving. (Volk, 2021)

Here is what the media leaves behind: for a significant number of young people, the question of when you become an adult is not a philosophical dilemma. It is answered for you, by the state. In Philadelphia and across the country, approximately 20,000 young people age out of the foster care system each year (Volk, 2021). At 18, or 21, they are released from the system and expected to magically get their life together without any resources or life skills. More than 30% will experience homelessness. Miracle did not have the luxury of pondering whether she felt like an adult. The system decided she was one, handed her a bus pass and a pamphlet, and wished her luck.

Evaluation

So when do you become an adult? My opinion, after reviewing the research and paying for my own rent and groceries for thirty days, is that we have created a damaging culture of adulthood that cannot be fully accomplished independently in this current age. What one person could do 40 years ago now requires the hard work and coordination of two people. A single 18-year-old who wants to be independent has very limited options. They can depend entirely on their family, they can work a single job, find multiple roommates and eat mostly ramen, or they can work two jobs, barely make rent and have some change left to go watch a movie. Neither of these options are easy or fulfilling but they give you one necessary and soul-fulfilling right, which is the freedom to dictate your life.

You can find almost all breaths of life in Philadelphia within walking distance of each other. There are those who sleep on the trains to avoid the cold because the state marked them as legal functioning adults when they turned 18, and there are those who receive DoorDash money and are still on their parents' phone plan. I do not begrudge any of these people their circumstances. I do think we owe it to our friends and ourselves to be more honest about what we mean when we say adulthood is complicated. For some of us complication is an existential crisis, for some it was never a problem at all but rather the tragedy that happened on a random Tuesday when the paperwork ran out.

I am not writing this from a place of hardship or as a calling for help. I am writing this as someone who once thought that until I got my "big boy" job, being independent was an impossible feat. I am now writing this piece sitting in my own studio apartment, watching the skyline from my own window and counting the sirens with my own lover on my side. I am someone who made a choice, faced the consequences of that choice and now am enjoying the fruits of that choice. What I am writing against is the culture of treating this leap of faith as impossible rather than a difficult obstacle everyone has to face. I understand for some people it is impossible, for others the problem was never the leaving, it was the staying.

Living on my own for the first time taught me the dos and nots of this city quite quickly. It is a place like others that does not wait for you to finally feel ready. The wheel keeps turning and turning, the wanderers lurking, the evil turning the city cold and the good turning the clock to keep the fire ablaze. The people walking, building, and choosing all at the same time.

The question isn't when you become an adult. It's when you stop asking for permission to be one.
